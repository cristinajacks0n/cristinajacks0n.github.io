PG&E Corporation (NYSE:PCG) is an energy holding company whose subsidiary, Pacific Gas and Electric Company, is a major utility company that provides electricity in Northern and Central California.

Company Overview

PG&E is a regulated utility and operates under the California Public Utilities Commission (CPUC), which caps PG&E's rate of return at 7.8%. Following its 2019 Chapter 11 bankruptcy, the result of being to blame for the 2017 and 2018 catastrophic wildfires, the company has undertaken a $63B multi-year infrastructure campaign.

The 2017–2018 wildfires proved that PG&E was due for a major overhaul — its infrastructure was outdated and dangerous. Now, with a new CEO and majority new board members, a plan for improvement centered around sustainability, keeping rates for customers low, and improving efficiency may set PG&E on a more stable future.

The purpose of this scenario analysis is to show potential future scenarios, and the probability of them occurring, that could affect PG&E.

The 4 scenarios, including the base case, are described below. From our base case, it is evident that the intrinsic share price differs from the currently traded market price, which demonstrates that investors have a different view on top of our current assumptions that might be driving the share price higher. With our scenario analysis, we might be able to describe the discrepancy between the two prices.

Base Case Description

The base case reflects a conservative estimate of PG&E's stated plans as described in their investor presentations, as well as reflecting current analyst sentiment from reliable sources and analyzing current financial health. The base case reflects PG&E's ongoing $63B capex plan, the recent decreasing, yet still negative, cash flows, and consecutive years of zero major wildfires in PG&E's area. The base case reflects CPUC-authorized rate of return of 7.80% as a structural limit.

Operating cash flows grow steadily as the new base rate expansion of 9% is approved and AI/EV demand plus natural population growth adds incremental load. FCFs remain negative through ~2029, then begin recovering as capex plateaus. This scenario uses all assumption values directly from the Part A DCF model.

A 55% probability is assigned to this case due to moderate to high confidence that PG&E is taking the steps necessary to not cause another major wildfire.

PG&E is found liable for a third major wildfire event. The AB 1054 Wildfire Fund is exhausted. The company enters Chapter 11 for a second time. Governor Newsom invokes state authority — embedded in the 2020 reorganization plan — to convert PG&E into a public utility. All shareholder equity is wiped out.

Bear Case A — Description

In this bear case, a pessimistic scenario, another wildfire occurs and PG&E is, once again, found to be liable. In 2019, California governor Gavin Newsom threatened a public takeover, making PG&E a state-owned utility (Cal Matters). In this case, there is a state fund to help with the liability that wildfires pose to electricity companies — the AB 1054 Wildfire Fund — yet this fund does not come close to covering the damages from 2017, and due to rising climate risk, a future wildfire might cause much larger damages.

Bear Case A — Analysis

This case is only 5% likely, due to steps PG&E has taken for wildfire mitigation and the fact that the company now has access to the AB 1054 Wildfire Fund as a first-line defense. Though the probability is low, it poses a high risk of having the company fully dissolved.

03 — Bear Case B
External Catastrophe
Not PG&E's Fault
25% probability

A major earthquake, which California is overdue for, a potential coastal tsunami, an unprecedented wind event, or geopolitical disruption causes widespread damage to PG&E's grid. In this bear case, PG&E is not found liable — but must absorb $8–10B in emergency capex on top of its existing plan. During this time, the OCF collapses during recovery, and debt servicing becomes severely strained.

Bear Case B

The key distinction from Bear A is that now, PG&E is not liable, so its equity isn't wiped out. Emergency repairs ($8–10B) on top of the existing $63B plan severely strain the balance sheet. This is a potential case given California's USGS seismic risk, climate change which increases the variability and severity of extreme weather events, and the possibility of geopolitical conflict and its effects on infrastructure. Given current events and climate risk coupled with PG&E's wildfire mitigation steps, this case is assigned a 25% probability.

Bear Case B — Analysis

In this Bear Case B, PG&E has survived but has to scale up its CapEx even more in response to the emergency situation. The cost of equity rises, raising the WACC. The higher WACC of 6.95% lowers both the PV of FCFs and the terminal value from $13.69B to $5.20B — an $8.49B difference. The $61.19B in net debt leaves zero cushion for unplanned capital calls, and the company must refinance and look for more debt or further assistance from the state. In this case, the AB Wildfire Fund can provide some relief.

Northern California becomes the epicenter of US AI infrastructure buildout. PG&E signs long-term power agreements with data center and AI companies centered in Silicon Valley, EV adoption and electrification accelerates under California mandates, and potentially remaining federal IRA credits reduce the effective tax burden. This drives OCF growth well above historical rates, CapEx spending slows, and FCF pushes toward positive territory faster than the base case.

Bull Case — Description

This bull case reflects the optimistic scenario for PG&E. As stated in PG&E Q4 2025 earnings, they expect 3.6 GW of data center projects to be instated in final engineering. Further, California's EV mandate requires 100% new car sales to be electric by 2035, and if this remains the trajectory, it will require significantly more load from PG&E and the grid to power transportation. Lastly, the IRA clean energy credits reduce the effective tax rate.

Discussion
Question 1 — Which scenario is most likely, and why?

The most likely scenario is the base case at 55% probability. This is because PG&E has had three consecutive years of zero major wildfires, has committed to and so far followed through on their $63B investment plan. Additionally, the CPUC has pre-approved their rate base expansion of 9% each year. However, it is also likely that due to climate risks, PG&E faces further disruption to their currently outdated infrastructure before the overhaul is finished.

Question 2 — What probability do you assign to each scenario, and why?

The base case is assigned 55% probability due to moderate to high confidence that PG&E is taking the steps necessary to avoid another major wildfire. PG&E has had three consecutive years of zero major wildfires, has committed to and so far followed through on its $63B investment plan, and the CPUC has pre-approved a rate base expansion of 9% per year — providing a structurally stable revenue floor. Bear A is assigned only 5% probability. PG&E now has access to the AB 1054 Wildfire Fund as a first-line defense, and the steps taken for wildfire mitigation reduce the likelihood of a third liability event. While Governor Newsom threatened a public takeover in 2019, his stance had cooled significantly by January 2026. The outcome is severe — equity goes to zero — but the likelihood of reaching that point is low given current trajectory. Bear B is assigned 25% probability because California's seismic risk, climate-driven increases in the severity of extreme weather events, and the possibility of geopolitical conflict affecting infrastructure are real and non-zero over any decade-long horizon. The key distinction is that PG&E would not be liable in this scenario, which is why the probability is higher than Bear A — the triggering event is outside PG&E's control and therefore harder to mitigate. The bull case is assigned 15% probability. The underlying drivers — PG&E's 3.6 GW data center pipeline in final engineering, California's EV mandate requiring 100% electric new car sales by 2035, and IRA clean energy credits — are real and already in motion. However, the timing and magnitude of their impact on FCF is uncertain, and CPUC approval processes create structural delays between demand growth and revenue recognition.

Question 3 — What does this analysis imply about investing in PG&E?

This analysis shows that investors might have something else they are looking at when it comes to valuing PG&E. It is interesting that the current market share price is $18.36 when the probability-weighted intrinsic is −$18.10, creating a ~$36 gap. It seems like the market is not pricing PG&E on a DCF basis. Instead, investors may be weighing their own models independent of past trends, given that PG&E has a completely new board and management group. Additionally, there is a chance that not even our bull case is correctly estimating the potential demand increase that AI poses on the California utility company.
